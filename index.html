01 · Mandate 임무

Mandate

The instinct will be to skip research and start designing templates. I'm asking you not to. Templates are the easy part. The strategy underneath them is what determines whether the work compounds or not. If we pick the wrong pillars now, the templates will render the wrong thing forever, and the year of posting that follows will all sit on a foundation that doesn't hold.

Your first week is dedicated to one job: figure out who actually wins on social as a personal voice in the US wellness, health, and contemplative space, and figure out why. Everything else, every template you design, every pipeline decision you make, every starter post we write together, is built on what you learn this week.

This is not a content calendar. It's not a marketing brief. We are building the long-term founder authority that will eventually carry every brand AVO launches, starting with Pheeew and continuing for years. That's a serious thing to build, and serious things deserve evidence underneath them, not instinct.

The principle to keep in mind throughout: authentic alignment with me is necessary but not sufficient. The categories we land on need to map to what real people actually want to follow over years, not what feels right in a brainstorm.

알렉스와의 진정성 있는 정렬은 필수이지만 그것만으로는 충분하지 않다. 카테고리는 사람들이 수년간 진심으로 팔로우하고 싶어하는 것에 부합해야 한다.
02 · Research Questions 다섯 가지 질문

Research Questions

There are five questions that need real answers by the end of the week. Each one feeds a specific decision later. Take each one seriously enough to write a short essay on it (one to two pages), with sources, not a paragraph.

Why do people follow personal accounts, and why do they stay?

This is the psychological foundation. Without it, every other decision is guesswork. We need to know what emotional need a follow actually serves, whether that's validation, learning, community, escapism, aspiration, or parasocial intimacy. We need to understand the difference between an account someone follows and stays with for years and one they follow on impulse and unfollow within a week. How does the parasocial bond form, and what breaks it? What signals trust in 2026, and what signals fakeness, and how does the audience detect either one?

What kinds of content build long retention versus short engagement spikes?

A viral post and a loyal follower are not the same thing, and we want followers who stay. So we need to know which categories have the shortest half-life (probably trends, hot takes, quote cards, news reactions) and which ones compound (probably education, frameworks, recurring rituals, longitudinal stories). What's the ratio of evergreen to timely content for accounts that grow steadily over years? And critically, are there content categories that drive engagement in the short term but actively erode credibility over time?

What does credibility look like in this specific space?

The US wellness and contemplative space has a credibility crisis, which is part of why there's an opportunity here. We need to know who the most-trusted voices are right now, and how they earned that trust. We need to know the credibility-destroying patterns: selling courses, MLM ties, undisclosed sponsorships, supplements with no evidence behind them. We need to understand how voices with religious or contemplative grounding land in 2026 culture compared to five years ago. Where is the line between credible-curious and crank? Between earnest and corny? And how important is formal credentialing (MD, RD, PhD) versus the authority that comes from lived practice?

What carousel structure and visual patterns actually work for personal voice?

This is the form question, and it shapes every template you build. We are automating carousels specifically, so the study has to be filtered through that lens. Cover slide patterns that earn the swipe versus ones people scroll past. Optimal slide count for retention (three, five, seven, ten) and how it varies by content type. Image-to-text balance per slide. Caption length and what role the caption plays relative to the carousel. End-card patterns that drive saves and shares. Hook patterns that work specifically for personal accounts as opposed to influencer or brand accounts. And one I want you to pay attention to: how important is showing face and personality in carousels, versus going all-text or all-photo.

Where is the underserved space we can occupy?

This is the white-space question, and it might be the most important of the five. We don't want to be voice number 847 in a saturated lane. So we need to know what's overproduced right now (the saturation map), what's underproduced (the gap map), and where my actual life happens to overlap with one of those gaps. Korean and East Asian wellness tradition uniquely offers things US audiences want but don't have a clear voice for. A Christian-curious or contemplative voice is rare in a wellness space that's been heavily New Age and mystical. A CPG-builder perspective is different from a pure wellness-influencer perspective. Which of these openings is most defensible, and which one most authentically maps to who I already am?

03 · Sources & Methods 출처와 방법

Sources & Methods

Use four primary research methods this week, in roughly this order.

Account audit

Pick somewhere between 15 and 25 personal accounts in the wellness, health, food, or contemplative space, including the deep-study and curated references in sections 5 and 6. For each account, log the post count over the last 90 days, categorize their content by type, note their engagement rate, and pull their growth trajectory if you can find it (Social Blade and ViewStats both work). The output is a spreadsheet that lets you see the relationship between content category and engagement at a glance.

Comment archaeology

For your top five reference accounts, read the comments on their highest-engagement carousels of the last six months. Don't skim. Read what people are actually saying. What needs are being met. What language they use. What gets replied to and what just sits there. The output is a one-page synthesis of how the audience actually talks, which becomes a guide for our caption voice and our reply patterns later.

Saturation map

For each candidate category we might consider (food, verse, news, education, practice, story, anything else), sample 30 or more accounts and assess saturation. Where is the same content being made by 50 people? Where is it being made by 5? The output is a heatmap showing crowded versus open lanes, which directly informs the differentiation question.

Long-form synthesis

Read three to five long-form pieces (Substack essays, podcast transcripts, books) on personal brand psychology, parasocial dynamics, or wellness culture. The reading list below is a starting point, not a prescription. Distill principles from them, not tactics. Tactics get outdated. Principles travel.

Reading list

For books, look at Show Your Work by Austin Kleon, Trust Me, I'm Lying by Ryan Holiday, The Wellness Trap by Christy Harrison, Strangers Drowning by Larissa MacFarquhar for moral-life voices, and anything Wendell Berry has written on attention. For newsletters and podcasts, the Casey Means newsletter, the Maintenance Phase podcast (especially for skeptical wellness audience patterns), Derek Thompson's Plain English, and Ted Gioia's The Honest Broker for cultural pattern reading. For data, the annual Edelman Trust Barometer, Pew Research on US religion and wellness, and Mintel reports on wellness consumer trends from 2024 to 2026. For voices on building authority in public, Casey Lewis (After School), Jasmine Sun, and Anu Atluru.

One warning. Marketing-blog content on "how to grow on Instagram" is mostly garbage. Almost all of it is written for SEO and recycles the same generic advice. Bias your reading toward primary observation (the actual account audits and comment data you do yourself) and toward people who have built audiences themselves and can speak from that.

"인스타그램 성장법" 류 마케팅 블로그 글은 거의 다 SEO용 재탕이다. 1차 관찰과 본인이 청중을 직접 키워본 사람들의 글을 우선시해라.
04 · Carousel Study Method 카루셀 연구 방법

Carousel Study Method

Most reference accounts post a mix of carousels, Reels, Stories, and single images. Our automation system is for carousels specifically, so the study has to be filtered through a carousel lens. If an account leans heavily on Reels, don't skip them. Find their last 10 to 15 carousel posts and study those.

For every carousel you study, log eight things. The table below gives you the framework.

Element What to log Why it matters
Cover slide Hook type (question, statement, statistic, image-only, name-of-thing). Text size relative to canvas. Whether they use face, object, or typography as the central element. The cover decides if anyone swipes. Without the swipe, slides 2 through 10 don't exist.
Slide count Total slides per post. Average across the account. Variance by content type. Different counts serve different content. A 3-slide rhythm reads differently from a 7-slide essay.
Slide rhythm Whether slides repeat in structure or each is unique. Whether the layout pulses (text then image then text then image). Rhythm controls swipe-completion rate. The reader either commits at slide 2 or drops out.
Text-image ratio Per slide and averaged: text-dominant, image-dominant, or balanced. Tells us what the audience reads versus scans, which directly affects template design.
Typography hierarchy How many type sizes per slide. Whether there's a clear primary, secondary, tertiary. Serif versus sans. Visual hierarchy is what makes a carousel readable while scrolling fast.
End slide pattern What the last slide does: CTA, soft close, repeat the question, brand mark only, save reminder. End slides drive saves and shares. The pattern matters more than people realize.
Caption pairing Caption length. Whether the caption expands the carousel content or repeats it. Whether there's a hook in the first line. The caption is where the algorithm reads context. Affects discovery.
Engagement signal Compare carousel posts to Reels posts on the same account. Which gets more comments, which gets more saves. Saves are what matter most for retention-focused accounts. Carousels generally win on saves.

By the end of the week you should have a spreadsheet of 30 to 50 specific carousel posts you studied (with links, account, slide count, hook type, end pattern, observed engagement), a one-page synthesis listing the five to seven carousel patterns that consistently work with examples, a one-page synthesis of three to five patterns to avoid with examples of why, and recommended cover-slide and end-slide vocabulary that we'll build into the AVO templates in week two.

07 · Anti-References 피해야 할 패턴

Anti-References

Equally important. Pick five to seven accounts that represent failure modes we want to design against. The table below outlines the patterns. Study them privately and document what specifically went wrong: when the credibility eroded, what content category caused it, and how the audience reacted.

Failure mode What it looks like Why we avoid it
Wellness influencer promo Constant supplement promo, MLM ties, course launches, undisclosed sponsorships Erodes credibility instantly. Audience trust dies on the first noticed ad.
Quote-card aesthetic Verses or quotes alone, no personal context, polished graphics with no voice Pinterest-grade. Indistinguishable from 50,000 other accounts. Nothing to follow for.
Hustle and outcome promises "Lose 20 lbs in 30 days," "Manifest your ideal life," "5 things that changed everything" Outcome-promise content has the shortest credibility half-life of any format.
Crank and pseudoscience Anti-vaccine messaging, citing discredited studies, raw water, fluoride conspiracies Burns serious-audience credibility for a niche audience that tips into harm.
AI-generated visible Obviously AI imagery, AI-written captions, generic stock-feeling content The 2026 audience detects this immediately. It reads as low-effort, even if it isn't.
Founder-content sloppiness "Here's how I closed my round." Founder humblebrag. Money talk. Wrong audience for AVO. Founder-content audiences and wellness audiences don't overlap.
Trend-chasing Every viral audio, every meme format, every passing trend Builds an audience that follows for novelty, not voice. The audience leaves with the trend.
08 · Cultural Context 문화적 맥락

Cultural Context

You need to understand the cultural moment we're entering. This isn't background reading. It directly determines which voice will land and which will feel dated within six months. Below is a quick map of where US wellness culture sits as of 2026, broken into rising trends, saturated lanes, and underserved spaces.

Rising

Microbiome health has fully crossed into the mainstream. Tim Spector's Zoe, Seed DS-01, and Pendulum have all trained the audience to be more sophisticated about prebiotics versus probiotics versus postbiotics. Gut health is no longer a niche topic; it's baseline literacy.

Longevity, sometimes branded as "Don't Die," has been normalized by Bryan Johnson and the gateway provided by Peter Attia's Outlive. The audience for protocol-driven content is large and growing.

Tradlife and regenerative culture is rising on a different axis: homesteading aesthetic, the raw milk debate, regenerative agriculture, real food traditions. This crosses political lines in interesting ways and is one of the more dynamic spaces in wellness right now.

There's a Christian and contemplative renaissance happening in parallel. Call it a vibe shift toward seriousness. Increased interest in liturgy, in Stoicism, in contemplative practice. Substack writers, podcasts, and books are all leaning in. This is real and growing, not a fad.

Saturated

Cold plunge and breathwork content has hit peak saturation. The Wim Hof aesthetic is everywhere, and men's health and testosterone optimization is one of the most crowded spaces in all of wellness right now. Hard to differentiate, easy to look like a hundred other guys.

The "That Girl" wellness aesthetic (Pilates, matcha, slow morning routine content) is heavily produced and commercially saturated. Everything is on-brand, nothing is differentiated.

Wellness skepticism is its own crowded category now. Maintenance Phase, Christy Harrison, and The Atlantic Health represent a real audience hungry for content that critiques wellness culture from the inside, but the lane is filling fast.

Underserved

Asian wellness traditions for a US audience is open. Maangchi covers Korean food beautifully, but there's almost no one bringing TCM, Korean herbalism, or Ayurveda to a US audience with both intellectual rigor and a personal voice. This is one of the clearest openings.

The builder-and-practitioner combination is also open. Most wellness voices are practitioners only. Most founders are too busy or too commercially-coded to be effective content creators. The voice of someone who is actually building clean CPG and actually living the practice is rare. That's me.

Spiritual-curious without dogma is open. Most spiritual content sits at one of two poles: hard-religious or hard-New-Age. The thoughtful, faith-curious, intellectually honest middle is mostly empty, and that's a place I could occupy honestly.

The strategic question for you to answer in the synthesis: where does my actual life overlap with the underserved spaces? That overlap is the position. Find it through research, not through guessing.

알렉스의 실제 삶과 비어 있는 자리가 겹치는 지점이 우리의 전략적 위치다. 추측이 아니라 리서치로 찾자.
09 · Synthesis Framework 종합 프레임워크

Synthesis Framework

By Friday you should have findings. By the following Monday you should have a synthesis that translates findings into recommendations I can act on. The synthesis needs to answer six questions, in this order.

Position. Where in the US wellness and contemplative space does AVO sit, given the saturation map and given who I actually am? This is the most important answer of the six because everything else flows from it.

Pillars. What three to five content pillars best serve that position? For each pillar, you need evidence from the research that the pillar has audience demand and is not fully saturated. No pillar makes the cut on instinct alone.

Voice. What five to seven voice principles emerged from your study of the reference and anti-reference accounts? Frame them as do's and don'ts and tie each one back to a specific observation.

Carousel format. What carousel structure consistently outperforms in this space? Cover slide patterns, body slide rhythm, optimal slide count, end card. This becomes the spec for the templates you build in week two.

Cadence. What posting frequency produces sustained growth without burnout, based on the long-term reference accounts you observed? Daily is too much. Weekly might be too little. Find the actual right answer.

Differentiation. What two to three things will be uniquely AVO that nothing else in the space currently does? This is the answer that should make me say "I wouldn't have thought of that."

When findings conflict

You'll hit conflicts. One reference does X, another does the opposite. When that happens, use this hierarchy in order: long-term retention beats short-term engagement, credibility-building beats growth-hacking, sustainable for me beats theoretically optimal, and underserved beats proven. If a tactic drives a viral spike but the accounts that use it churn audience faster, reject it. If a tactic grows numbers but erodes trust, reject it. If a recommendation requires me to be someone I'm not (extroverted, religious-traditionalist, science-credentialed), reject it. And if two paths both work, the less crowded one builds more durable distinct authority.

10 · Deliverable 결과물

Deliverable

One document, somewhere between five and eight pages. Format is up to you (PDF, slide deck, Notion page, whatever lets you communicate clearest). It needs to contain the following: a one-paragraph methodology summary, your one-to-two-paragraph answer to each of the five research questions with citations, the saturation map as a visual, an audit table of 15 to 25 accounts (including all six curated references), your carousel pattern findings (five to seven that work, three to five to avoid, with examples from real posts), your anti-pattern list with evidence, your recommended position with reasoning, your three to five recommended pillars with research backing, your five to seven recommended voice principles with examples, your carousel format and cadence specification, and your two to three differentiators.

Bring it to a one-hour review session with me. We'll discuss, agree on direction, and you'll start template design the following Monday.

What this is not: a marketing-blog summary, a list of "10 wellness influencers to watch," or a generic content calendar. It's original research-driven strategy. The bar is that I read it and think "I would not have arrived at this on my own."

마케팅 블로그 요약이 아니다. 일반적인 콘텐츠 캘린더가 아니다. 알렉스가 "이건 나 혼자선 떠올리지 못했겠다"고 말할 수 있는 리서치 기반 전략이어야 한다.

12 · Constraints 절대 원칙

Constraints

Some things are fixed regardless of what the research finds. These are about who I am, not about what the audience wants. The first one below overrides everything else in this document.

The First Rule

AVO is not a guru.

I do not know more than I know. We do not teach. We do not claim expertise we have not earned. We do not overstate findings, we do not promise outcomes, and we do not position as authority where there is none.

What we are instead: a practitioner sharing the path, not the summit. A student in public, not a teacher in disguise. A reader, an eater, a builder paying attention, not a coach handing out frameworks.

The test for any post: would a real expert in the field cringe reading this? If yes, kill it. If a sentence claims more than the source supports, rewrite it. If a caption would feel uncomfortable to read aloud to someone with actual credentials in the domain, it doesn't get posted.

제1원칙 · AVO는 구루가 아니다.
알렉스는 자신이 아는 것 이상을 안다고 말하지 않는다. 우리는 가르치지 않는다. 얻지 않은 권위를 주장하지 않는다. 발견을 과장하지 않는다. 결과를 약속하지 않는다. 권위가 없는 곳에서 권위자처럼 행동하지 않는다. 우리는 정상이 아닌 길 위의 실천자다. 가르치는 사람이 아닌 공개된 학생이다. 코치가 아닌, 주의를 기울이는 사람이다. 이 원칙을 통과하지 못하는 글은 올리지 않는다.

Voice

No em-dashes. They've become an AI-content tell, and breaking the habit forces tighter sentences. Use commas, colons, and periods instead. No New Age vocabulary: "manifest," "vibrate higher," "high-vibe," anything in that family is rejected regardless of audience preference. No outcome promises. "Lose X by Y," "Cure your gut in 30 days," "Achieve mental clarity" content is banned even if research shows it works. And no teaching posture. We share what we observe. We don't teach. "Here's how to do X" gets reframed as "Here's what I tried and what happened."

Authority

No overclaiming. We don't speak with more certainty than the evidence warrants. "Studies show" requires the actual study link. "It's well known" is banned phrasing. No expertise theater. We never imply credentials I don't have, never frame personal opinion as professional consensus, and never adopt a doctor or nutritionist or scientist tone outside of cited research. Authority is borrowed, not invented. When citing research, name the researcher. When sharing a protocol, name the source. When quoting wisdom, name the speaker. And cite sources always: any claim, any study, any statistic gets attribution. If we can't cite it, we don't post it.

Identity

Public language uses LA and US, not internal references. "Silver Lake," "East-side," and any other neighborhood-specific name stays internal-only. Practitioner posture, always. Even if guru-content gets more engagement (and it might), we don't do it. There's only one acceptable posture in this account, and it's the one of a person paying attention in public.
